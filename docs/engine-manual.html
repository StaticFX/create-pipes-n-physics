Engineexplained.

A field guide to how water actually moves through your pipes — every word defined, every number derived. Built for the curious plumber, not the mathematician.

Your pipe network is an electrical circuit. Water height is voltage, pipes are resistors, pumps are batteries. Every game tick, the engine solves the circuit once to decide how much water should move — then moves it.

01 — THE BIG IDEA

Buckets, straws, and a little bit of electricity

Imagine buckets connected by straws. A bucket with more water — or one sitting higher up — wants to push water out. A long thin straw fights that push; a short fat one barely resists. A pump is you blowing into the straw. Water flows from high want to low want until everything balances.

The whole engine is that picture, made precise by borrowing three parts from a circuit. Learn these three swaps and you understand the model:

⌁ Capacitor

Tank

stores fluid · has a water line

A bucket. Holds volume. Its capacitance is how many millibuckets it takes to raise the surface by one block. Wide tank = high capacitance.

⏚ Resistor

Pipe run

a path · stores nothing

A straw. It does not hold water — it only decides how fast water moves between tanks. Long & thick & sticky = slow.

⎓ Battery (EMF)

Pump

adds push · one-way doors

Blows into the straw, adding head that wasn't there. Has check valves (no backflow) and a top speed set by its RPM.

The one rule Water flows from high "want" to low "want" until balanced. The fancy name for "want" is head — and head is just how high the water's surface is sitting. That word does almost all the work in this engine, so it gets its own chapter.
02 — THE MASTER CONCEPT

Head is a height, not a pressure

Head is the single most important number in the engine. It is the Y-coordinate of the top surface of the fluid. Higher surface → more push. Water always flows from high head toward low head.

Why "surface" and not "amount"?

Because that's the trick that makes a thin pipe and a fat tank settle correctly. Connect them at the bottom and they stop when their surfaces line up — even though the fat tank holds far more fluid. Engineers call that communicating vessels, and you get it for free by defining head as surface height. Drag the level below and watch it happen:

Gravity is not special-cased Nobody wrote "if higher, flow down." Because head is elevation, a tall tank automatically has more head than a low one, so water falls on its own. Gravity feeds, water towers, siphons, draining — all emerge from this one definition.
03 — THE VOCABULARY

Every word, defined once

The spec is dense with jargon. Here is the entire dictionary — kid version on the left isn't needed; each term gets the real meaning in one line.

Head
Height of the fluid's top surface: baseY + fillHeight. Flow goes high → low. The master quantity.
Capacitance
How much fluid it takes to raise a tank's surface by one block. Wide tank = big. Pipes have none (they store nothing).
Conductance
How easily fluid moves through a pipe — the opposite of resistance. Big = fast. Set by length & viscosity.
Viscosity
Stickiness. Water ≈ 1000, honey ≈ 5000. Higher viscosity lowers conductance → slower steady rate (never travel time).
EMF
The pump's "battery push," in blocks of extra head: |RPM| × 0.25. A 64-RPM pump can lift water 16 blocks.
Node
An important spot: a tank, a pump, a junction (T/cross), or an open pipe mouth. Where decisions happen.
Edge
A boring straight pipe run between two nodes — every 2-connection cell is squashed into one edge to shrink the math.
Junction
Where pipes meet. Rule: flow in = flow out (Kirchhoff). Nothing piles up, because pipes can't store fluid.
Open end
A pipe mouth in open air. Default: a one-way drain (spills, never reclaims). Optionally a vacuum intake.
Check valve
A one-way door (built into pumps). Fluid may pass one direction only — enforced after solving.
BoundaryColumn
The code's model of a tank/open-end as a vertical column: knows its baseY, height, capacity and fill fraction.
Tick
One game heartbeat — 20 per second. Each tick: solve the network once, move fluid once.
Crest
The highest cell of a pipe run. If it rises too far above reachable head, the column "breaks" (siphon limit).
04 — HOW FLOW IS CALCULATED

The formula at the heart of it all

Everything reduces to one line. For a single pipe between point A and point B, the flow per tick is:

flow through one pipe q = G × ( hA − hB + emf )
q — flow rate (mB/tick) G — conductance (slipperiness) hA, hB — the two heads emf — pump push

Read it in plain English: (hA − hB) is the difference in want between the two ends. Add any pump push. Multiply by conductance to scale it — a slippery pipe turns that difference into a torrent, a sticky one into a trickle.

Direction is never stored Every tick the engine just recomputes q. Positive → water flows A→B; negative → B→A. That's why the same formula produces gravity drain, equalization, siphons, and uphill pumping — only the head values differ.

What goes into conductance (G)

slipperiness of the pipe G = 120 × (1000 / viscosity) ÷ (length + 1)

120 is PIPE_CONDUCTANCE, a tuning constant. 1000 ÷ viscosity makes water full-speed (1000/1000 = 1) and honey 5× slower (1000/5000 = 0.2). ÷ (length + 1) means a long pipe flows slower — but, crucially, never stops. Distance only lowers the rate.

What goes into the pump push (emf)

the battery emf = |RPM| × 0.25 blocks of head per RPM

A pump spinning at 64 RPM adds 16 blocks of head — it can lift water 16 blocks above the source surface. It also has an internal speed cap (≈ RPM × 1.0 mB/tick) so it can't shove infinitely fast no matter how big the head gap.

From one pipe to the whole network

A single pipe is easy. But in a real network the head at every junction depends on every other junction at the same instant — it's circular, so you can't solve them one at a time. Instead the engine solves them all at once as one system of equations:

solve the whole circuit, one tick ahead ( C/dt + L ) · h′ = ( C/dt ) · h + EMF
Inputs

h = heads right now. C = each tank's capacitance (how big it is). L = the "wiring table" — which nodes connect, weighted by conductance. EMF = pump pushes.

Output

h′ = the heads one tick from now. Plug them back into q = G(hA−hB+emf) and you have every pipe's flow at once.

You don't need the linear algebra. The equation just asks: "given where the water is now, and how everything is wired, where will it be next tick?" A computer solves it with Gaussian elimination (small networks, ≤128 nodes) or conjugate gradient (big ones).

05 — WHY IT NEVER EXPLODES

Implicit Euler: solve for the future, don't guess it

Euler just means "step the simulation forward one tick." There are two ways to do it, and the choice is the difference between a stable engine and a sloshing disaster.

✗ Explicit

Guess from now

Use the current heads to move water. Simple — but it overshoots: pushes too much, the level shoots past balance, then over-corrects back. Forever. This oscillation killed the v1 engine.

✓ Implicit

Solve for the future

Solve for the end-of-tick heads where everything is already consistent. It cannot overshoot — mathematically it always settles toward balance, never past it.

Watch them race to balance

Two connected tanks start far apart and try to equalize. Press play. Same starting point, two stepping methods — one settles, one sloshes.

Explicit — overshoots
Implicit — settles
Load-bearing rule The spec says in bold: do not replace implicit stepping with explicit. The whole engine's stability rests on it. The active-set loop (fixing one-way valves) and the one-shot crest gate (siphons) sit on top of this stable solve — they're touch-ups, not the foundation.
06 — ONE TICK, START TO FINISH

The seven steps the engine runs, 20× a second

Up until the very last step the engine is read-only — it only thinks. Then it acts, both-or-neither, never half a transfer.

1

Wake up EngineTickHandler

Pipes mark themselves dirty; pump flips and speed changes also wake sleeping networks. Idle networks nap up to 20 ticks — unless they hold a running pump, which re-checks every 4.

2

Build the graph GraphBuilder

Walk the pipes outward (BFS). Squash every 2-connection cell into an edge; tanks, pumps, junctions and open mouths become nodes. A 30-block straight run becomes one edge.

3

Collect columns

Read every tank's surface height (its head) and its size (capacitance). Multiblock tanks resolve to one column. Open ends pin their head at the mouth.

4

Assemble branches

For each edge: conductance from length & viscosity, EMF from pump flanks, one-way rules from check valves & valve throttles, and the crest (highest cell) for siphon checks.

5

Solve NetworkSolver

One implicit-Euler linear solve → all end-of-tick heads. The active-set loop zeroes any branch flowing a forbidden direction and re-solves. The one-shot crest gate tapers siphon flow.

6

Plan transfers

Net flow per tank, clamped by MAX_FLOW_PER_ENDPOINT (256), the lip-drain cap, and a SIMULATE probe. Pair sources to sinks — only within the same connected island.

7

Apply FluidEngine.apply

Re-resolve handlers (the world may have changed), simulate drain + fill, then execute both or neither. The only step that touches real fluid.

07 — THE THREE BIG SHIFTS

What makes this engine feel different

① Pipes hold no water

They are pure conduits. Only tanks, basins and open ends actually store fluid; the pipe just sets how fast it moves between them. The fluid you see in a pipe is painted on afterward to match the solved flow — it's a rendering, not storage.

② Reach is bounded by height, not distance

A long horizontal pipe just flows slower — it never stops. But you genuinely cannot push water higher than source surface + RPM × 0.25 blocks. Downhill is free; lifting costs RPM. That's the entire puzzle of the mod.

③ Gas flips gravity

A lighter-than-air fluid uses the mirror-image head fillHeight − baseY, so it pools in the highest tank as hard as water pools in the lowest. Same engine, sign flipped — no special case.

08 — QUICK REFERENCE

The constants that tune the world

These are the server-config knobs. Change them and the physics re-tunes — no code edits.

PIPE_CONDUCTANCE
120 — base mB/tick per block of head difference. The master "how fast pipes are."
PUMP_HEAD_PER_RPM
0.25 — blocks of lift each RPM buys. 64 RPM → 16 blocks of reach.
PUMP_FLOW_PER_RPM
1.0 — caps a pump's throughput at ≈ RPM mB/tick, no matter the head gap.
SUCTION_LIMIT
8 — how far above the local head a crest may rise before the column breaks (siphon limit).
MAX_FLOW_PER_ENDPOINT
256 — hard ceiling on mB moved through any one tank per tick.
ENABLE_ENGINE
true — the master switch. False reverts cleanly to stock Create transport.
